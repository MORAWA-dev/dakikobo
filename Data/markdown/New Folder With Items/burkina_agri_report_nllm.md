## 1.0  Executive  Overview  of  Burkina  Faso's  Agricultural  Sector
The  agricultural  sector  is  the  cornerstone  of  Burkina  Faso's  economy  and  social  fabric,  representing  the  primary  source  of  livelihood  for  the  vast  majority  of  its  population.  As  a  low-income,  landlocked  West  African  nation,  its  path  toward  sustainable  development,  food  security,  and  poverty  reduction  is  fundamentally  linked  to  the  performance  and  resilience  of  its  agricultural  systems.  A  strategic  understanding  of  this  sector  is  therefore  essential  for  any  initiative  aimed  at  fostering  national  progress  and  improving  the  well-being  of  its  citizens.
The  sector's  profile  is  defined  by  a  reliance  on  small-scale,  subsistence  farming  operating  under  significant  climatic  and  environmental  pressures.  Its  core  characteristics  are  summarized  below:
●  Contribution  to  GDP:  Varies  significantly  by  source  and  year,  with  estimates  ranging  from  20.4%  to  nearly  40%,  underscoring  the  sector's  foundational  but  fluctuating  role  in  the  national  economy.  ●  Workforce  Employment:  It  employs  between  80%  and  over  90%  of  the  country's  active  population.  ●  Dominant  Farm  Size:  The  landscape  is  dominated  by  small-scale  farms  of  less  than  5  hectares.  ●  Primary  Agricultural  Products:  Key  staple  and  cash  crops  include  sorghum,  millet,  maize,  cotton,  rice,  cowpea  (niébé),  and  sesame.  ●  National  Context:  Burkina  Faso  is  classified  as  a  low-income,  landlocked  country.
The  agricultural  economy  is  driven  by  a  mix  of  commodities  serving  distinct  markets,  from  local  consumption  to  international  trade.  Cotton  has  historically  been  a  vital  source  of  foreign  currency,  but  a  range  of  other  products  are  critical  for  both  domestic  food  supply  and  regional  commerce.

## 2.0  Policy,  Governance,  and  Institutional  Framework
National  and  regional  policies  are  critical  for  guiding  agricultural  investment,  enhancing  productivity,  and  building  resilience  in  Burkina  Faso.  This  framework  provides  the  essential  structure  for  public  and  private  interventions,  setting  strategic  priorities  and  defining  the  legal  environment  for  farmers,  agribusinesses,  and  investors.
National  Strategic  Framework
The  government  has  established  several  key  strategies  to  direct  the  sector's  growth  and  development:
1.  Strategy  for  Accelerated  Growth  and  Sustainable  Development  (SCADD):  This  overarching  national  framework  aims  to  boost  overall  economic  growth  and  reduce  poverty,  with  agriculture  as  a  central  pillar.  2.  National  Plan  for  Agricultural  Investment  (PNIA):  As  the  primary  implementation  tool  for  agricultural  policy,  the  PNIA  is  structured  around  seven  strategic  axes:  ○  Sustainable  land  management  and  climate  change  adaptation.  ○  Improved  water  management.  ○  Sustainable  development  of  agricultural  operations.  ○  Improved  management  of  other  shared  resources.  ○  Development  of  agricultural  value  chains  and  market  promotion.  ○  Prevention  and  management  of  food  crises  and  other  calamities.  ○  Institutional  strengthening.
Regional  Policy  Influence
The  sector  is  also  shaped  by  the  Economic  Community  of  West  African  States  (ECOWAS)  Agricultural  Policy  (ECOWAP) .  This  regional  framework  seeks  to  harmonize  agricultural  development  across  member  states  and  has  three  primary  lines  of  action:  increasing  agricultural  productivity  and  competitiveness,  implementing  an  intra-community  trade  regime,  and  adapting  the  external  trade  regime  to  protect  and  promote  regional  markets.
Challenges  in  Policy  Implementation
Despite  a  comprehensive  policy  landscape,  the  implementation  of  agricultural  legislation  faces  significant  constraints  that  hinder  its  effectiveness.  Key  weaknesses  include:
●  The  lack  of  a  clear,  legally  agreed-upon  definition  of  an  "agricultural  enterprise,"  which  complicates  the  application  of  investment  codes.  ●  Poor  dissemination  of  legal  and  regulatory  texts,  especially  in  national  languages,  making  them  inaccessible  to  the  majority  of  the  farming  population.  ●  High  rates  of  illiteracy  among  farmers,  which  creates  a  barrier  to  understanding  their  rights  and  the  incentives  available  to  them.  ●  Dispersed  and  hard-to-access  legal  provisions,  which  make  it  difficult  for  investors  and  other  stakeholders  to  navigate  the  regulatory  environment.  ●  Authorization  requirements  for  investment  that  are  inconsistent  with  modern  international  standards,  creating  unnecessary  constraints.
Key  Institutions
Several  government  ministries  and  research  bodies  oversee  the  sector.  The  principal  government  entity  is  the  Ministère  de  l'Agriculture  et  des  Aménagements  Hydro-agricoles  (Ministry  of  Agriculture  and  Hydro-agricultural  Development).  On  the  research  front,  the  Institut  de  l’Environnement  et  de  Recherches  Agricoles  (INERA)  serves  as  Burkina  Faso's  leading  institute  for  agricultural  and  environmental  research,  playing  a  crucial  role  in  developing  and  adapting  new  technologies  and  practices.
While  Burkina  Faso's  policy  framework  is  ambitious  and  well-aligned  with  regional  goals,  its  effectiveness  is  severely  undermined  by  persistent  implementation  gaps.  The  combination  of  high  illiteracy  among  farmers  and  poor  dissemination  of  legal  texts  creates  a  significant  policy-to-practice  divide.  This  reality  underscores  a  critical  need  for  accessible,  digitally-enabled  advisory  services  and  legal  information  platforms  that  can  deliver  crucial  knowledge  directly  to  farmers  in  local  languages,  thereby  translating  national  strategy  into  on-the-ground  action.
## 3.0  Climate  Change  and  Environmental  Pressures
Climate  change  is  a  pervasive  and  widespread  threat  to  Burkina  Faso's  agricultural  system,  acting  as  a  primary  driver  of  vulnerability  for  millions  of  smallholder  farmers.  The  increasing  frequency  and  intensity  of  climate-related  hazards  undermine  food  security,  threaten  livelihoods,  and  necessitate  a  strategic  shift  toward  more  resilient  farming  systems.
Primary  Climate  Hazards
The  country  is  experiencing  a  number  of  clear  and  concerning  climate  trends  that  directly  impact  agriculture:
●  A  consistent  trend  of  declining  rainfall  and  increasing  temperatures  has  been  observed  since  the  1970s.  ●  The  frequency  of  extreme  weather  events,  particularly  droughts  and  floods,  has  risen,  disrupting  production  cycles.  ●  Climate  projections  indicate  that  semi-arid  areas  of  the  country  are  expected  to  become  more  arid  in  the  coming  years.
Impacts  on  Agricultural  Production
These  climatic  pressures  are  projected  to  have  significant  negative  effects  on  the  yields  of  both  staple  and  export  crops.  Modeling  shows  that  climate  change
will  negatively  affect  the  yields  of  staple  cereals  like  maize  and  sorghum.  Key  export  commodities  are  also  at  risk,  with  cotton  yields  projected  to  decline  by  4%  to  7%.  Other  important  crops,  including  vegetables  and  sugarcane,  are  also  expected  to  perform  poorly.  However,  some  nutritionally  important  root  and  tuber  crops,  such  as  sweet  potato  and  yams,  have  demonstrated  relative  resilience  to  these  changes  and  may  offer  potential  for  expansion.
Environmental  Degradation
The  challenges  of  climate  change  are  compounded  by  severe  environmental  degradation  linked  to  both  climatic  pressures  and  agricultural  practices.  An  estimated  30%  of  the  country's  arable  lands  are  under  threat  of  degradation .  This  is  driven  by  a  combination  of  factors,  including  deforestation  for  agricultural  expansion,  overexploitation  of  soil  nutrients  without  adequate  replenishment,  and  soil  erosion  caused  by  more  intense  rainfall  events.
The  convergence  of  adverse  climate  trends  and  severe  environmental  degradation  poses  an  existential  threat  to  Burkina  Faso's  agricultural  base.  This  is  not  merely  an  incremental  challenge  but  a  fundamental  crisis  that  invalidates  traditional,  rainfed  production  models.  The  situation  mandates  a  complete  strategic  reorientation  toward  systemic  resilience,  making  the  widespread  adoption  of  integrated  approaches  like  Climate-Smart  Agriculture  an  imperative  for  survival,  not  just  an  option  for  improvement.
## 4.0  Climate-Smart  Agriculture  (CSA)  Investment  Strategy
Climate-Smart  Agriculture  (CSA)  is  an  integrated  approach  designed  to  guide  actions  needed  to  transform  and  reorient  agricultural  systems  to  effectively  support  development  and  ensure  food  security  in  a  changing  climate.  For  Burkina  Faso,  the  Climate-Smart  Agriculture  Investment  Plan  (CSAIP)  serves  as  the  nation's  primary  framework  for  building  a  productive,  resilient,  and  low-emission  agricultural  sector.
The  core  purpose  of  the  CSAIP  is  to  identify  and  prioritize  investments  that  align  with  the  three  internationally  recognized  pillars  of  CSA:
1.  Productivity:  Sustainably  increasing  agricultural  productivity  and  incomes.  2.  Resilience:  Adapting  and  building  resilience  to  climate  change.  3.  Mitigation:  Reducing  and/or  removing  greenhouse  gas  emissions,  where  possible.

Financing  Landscape  for  CSA
Mobilizing  capital  for  these  investments  is  a  central  challenge.  The  CSAIP  identifies  a  diverse  range  of  potential  financing  sources,  reflecting  the  need  for  a  multi-stakeholder  approach  that  combines  public,  private,  and  international  support.  Key  identified  sources  include:
●  Public:  The  Environmental  Intervention  Fund  (FIE),  Banque  Agricole  du  Burkina  Faso.  ●  International:  The  Global  Environment  Fund  (GEF),  the  Green  Climate  Fund  (GCF),  the  African  Development  Bank  (AfDB),  and  the  World  Bank.  ●  Private:  Opportunities  for  private  sector  investment  and  business  development  are  envisioned  across  all  priority  areas.
The  CSA  Investment  Plan  provides  a  clear  and  technically  sound  roadmap  for  building  a  resilient  agricultural  sector,  complete  with  prioritized  interventions.  However,  its  success  is  entirely  contingent  on  mobilizing  a  complex  and  large-scale  mix  of  public,  private,  and  international  finance.  The  significant  funding  gaps  identified  for  these  priority  packages  highlight  the  critical  need  for  de-risking  mechanisms  and  innovative  financial  instruments  capable  of  attracting  the  private  capital  required  for  transformative  impact.
## 5.0  The  Digital  Agriculture  Landscape  and  AI  Potential
Digital  technologies  hold  transformative  potential  for  modernizing  Burkina  Faso's  agriculture.  Their  adoption  represents  a  crucial  lever  for  overcoming  traditional  barriers  related  to  information  asymmetry,  market  access,  financial  inclusion,  and  climate  resilience.  By  connecting  farmers  to  timely  data,  new  markets,  and  innovative  services,  digital  tools  can  accelerate  the  sector's  transition  toward  greater  productivity  and  sustainability.
Current  State  of  Digital  Readiness
The  path  to  digital  transformation  is  marked  by  both  significant  barriers  and  emerging  opportunities.  A  realistic  assessment  of  the  landscape  reveals  the  following:
●  Barriers:  ○  Low  Digital  Literacy:  Overall  digital  literacy  in  the  country  is  estimated  at  just  9%,  posing  a  major  hurdle  for  the  adoption  of  complex  digital  tools.
○  Undeveloped  Infrastructure:  Information-communication  technology  networks  remain  relatively  undeveloped,  particularly  in  rural  areas.  ○  High  Costs:  The  cost  of  technology,  including  hardware  and  digital  platform  subscriptions,  can  be  prohibitive  for  many  smallholder  farmers.  ●  Enablers:  ○  Growing  Mobile  Connectivity:  Mobile  penetration  is  expanding,  creating  a  foundational  layer  for  delivering  digital  services.  ○  Government  Commitment:  The  government  has  established  plans  and  strategies  to  expand  ICT  infrastructure  and  promote  the  digital  economy.  ○  Local  Innovation:  A  nascent  ecosystem  of  local  AgriTech  initiatives,  innovation  hubs,  and  incubators  is  emerging.
Existing  Digital  Agriculture  Initiatives
Several  digital  platforms  and  services  are  already  active  in  Burkina  Faso,  providing  valuable  lessons  and  a  foundation  for  future  growth:
●  Viamo:  This  service  operates  a  "3-2-1"  platform  that  allows  farmers  to  access  information  on  market  prices  and  agricultural  best  practices  in  local  languages  through  call  centers,  catering  to  those  with  basic  mobile  phones  and  varying  literacy  levels.  ●  Farafina  Agri-Funding:  This  initiative  is  a  digital  crowdfunding  platform  specifically  dedicated  to  financing  agricultural  entrepreneurship,  connecting  local  projects  with  potential  investors.  ●  Cargitech  Sarl  &  Espace  Géomatique  Sarl:  These  local  companies  specialize  in  geospatial  technologies,  offering  services  in  Geographic  Information  Systems  (GIS),  remote  sensing,  and  precision  agriculture  to  support  more  data-driven  land  and  resource  management.
Potential  Applications  of  Artificial  Intelligence  (AI)
Drawing  from  the  broader  context  of  AI  in  Sub-Saharan  African  agriculture,  Artificial  Intelligence  offers  powerful  capabilities  to  address  some  of  the  sector's  most  persistent  challenges.  Potential  applications  include:
1.  Crop  and  Soil  Management  AI  can  analyze  data  from  sensors,  drones,  and  satellite  imagery  to  provide  real-time  insights  for  crop  monitoring.  AI-powered  mobile  applications,  such  as  PlantVillage's  Nuru-App,  can  help  farmers  diagnose  crop  diseases  directly  in  the  field.  This  enables  more  precise,  data-driven  decisions  on  the  optimal  use  of  water,  fertilizers,  and  pest  control  measures.
2.  Climate  Information  Services  By  applying  machine  learning  algorithms  to  vast  datasets  of  historical  weather  patterns  and  real-time  field  data,  AI  can  generate  more  accurate  and  localized  predictive  climate  insights.  These  enhanced  climate  information  services  are  crucial  for  helping  farmers  make  informed  decisions  about  planting  times,  crop  selection,  and  risk  management  in  the  face  of  increasing  climate  variability.  3.  Market  Access  and  Supply  Chain  AI  can  be  used  to  optimize  agricultural  supply  chains  by  improving  traceability,  forecasting  demand,  and  matching  supply  from  smallholders  with  buyers  more  efficiently.  This  can  reduce  post-harvest  losses,  increase  market  transparency,  and  help  farmers  secure  better  prices  for  their  products.  4.  Financial  Services  AI-powered  fintech  solutions  can  revolutionize  access  to  credit  and  insurance  for  smallholder  farmers.  By  analyzing  alternative  data  sources  to  assess  creditworthiness,  AI  can  overcome  the  challenge  of  farmers  lacking  formal  credit  histories  or  collateral.  This  facilitates  the  development  of  more  inclusive  financial  products  tailored  to  the  agricultural  sector.
While  digital  tools  and  AI  present  a  powerful  pathway  to  overcome  long-standing  barriers  in  Burkinabé  agriculture,  the  current  landscape  is  defined  by  a  profound  digital  divide.  Extremely  low  digital  literacy  and  undeveloped  rural  infrastructure  mean  that  advanced  solutions  risk  benefiting  only  a  small  minority.  This  implies  that  any  effective  digital  transformation  strategy  must  be  two-pronged,  focusing  simultaneously  on  developing  sophisticated  AI  applications  and  investing  in  foundational  digital  literacy  and  infrastructure  to  ensure  equitable  access  and  widespread  adoption.
## 6.0  Financial  Ecosystem  and  Investment  Climate
A  robust  and  inclusive  financial  ecosystem  is  a  critical  enabler  of  agricultural  transformation.  Access  to  finance  directly  determines  the  ability  of  farmers  and  agribusinesses  to  invest  in  improved  seeds,  fertilizers,  technology,  and  the  climate-resilient  practices  needed  to  modernize  the  sector.  However,  the  flow  of  capital  into  agriculture  remains  constrained  by  several  deep-rooted  challenges.
Barriers  to  Agricultural  Finance
Farmers  and  agricultural  enterprises  in  Burkina  Faso  face  numerous  obstacles  when  attempting  to  secure  financing:
●  A  widespread  lack  of  formal  collateral  and  documented  credit  histories  among  smallholder  farmers.  ●  Weaknesses  in  the  business  operations  and  economies  of  scale  of  many  microfinance  organizations.  ●  Low  levels  of  financial  literacy  among  rural  populations.  ●  A  perception  of  high  risk  associated  with  the  rainfed  agricultural  sector,  which  discourages  lending  from  commercial  banks.
Investment  Incentive  Structure
To  attract  private  investment,  the  government  has  established  an  Investment  Code  that  offers  a  tiered  system  of  incentives  based  on  the  size  of  the  investment  and  the  number  of  permanent  jobs  created.

Innovative  Financial  Solutions
Addressing  the  financing  gap  requires  moving  beyond  traditional  banking  and  exploring  innovative  mechanisms  that  are  better  suited  to  the  agricultural  context:
●  Credit  Bureaus:  There  is  a  recognized  need  to  improve  the  public  credit  registry  by  expanding  it  to  include  data  on  individuals  and  Small  and  Medium-sized  Enterprises  (SMEs).  This  would  help  build  credit  histories  and  reduce  the  risk  for  lenders.  ●  Capital  Markets:  Developing  deeper  capital  markets  could  offer  mechanisms  to  restructure  and  modernize  agriculture  by  enabling  agribusinesses  to  raise  capital  and  create  more  competitive  value  chains.
●  Leasing:  Equipment  leasing  represents  a  promising  form  of  finance,  as  it  does  not  require  farmers  to  provide  upfront  collateral  or  have  an  established  credit  history,  since  the  lessor  retains  ownership  of  the  asset.  ●  Digital  Financial  Services:  Mobile  finance  is  a  key  area  for  innovation.  Organizations  like  AGRA  are  supporting  the  development  of  flexible  financial  products,  delivered  via  mobile  platforms,  that  are  adapted  to  the  specific  needs  of  SMEs  and  farmers,  such  as  adapting  repayment  schedules  to  harvest  cycles.
The  financial  ecosystem  remains  the  primary  bottleneck  to  agricultural  modernization  in  Burkina  Faso,  as  the  high  perceived  risk  of  the  sector  and  farmers'  lack  of  traditional  collateral  exclude  the  vast  majority  from  formal  credit.  This  indicates  that  meaningful  transformation  requires  a  paradigm  shift  away  from  conventional  lending.  Prioritizing  innovative,  data-driven  models  such  as  digital  financial  services,  leasing,  and  alternative  credit  scoring  is  essential  to  accurately  price  risk  and  unlock  the  capital  needed  for  investment  in  climate  resilience  and  productivity  by  the  rural  majority.
## 7.0  Food  Security,  Social  Context,  and  International  Support
The  performance  of  Burkina  Faso's  agricultural  sector  is  directly  tied  to  the  food  security  and  social  well-being  of  its  population.  The  compounding  effects  of  climate  shocks,  environmental  degradation,  and  rising  conflict  translate  into  heightened  food  insecurity,  particularly  for  vulnerable  rural  households  whose  livelihoods  depend  on  agriculture.
Food  Insecurity  Snapshot
Acute  food  insecurity  remains  a  significant  challenge.  In  2019,  an  estimated  3.3  million  people  in  Burkina  Faso  suffered  from  food  insecurity.  The  analysis  and  classification  of  food  security  levels  in  the  country  are  conducted  using  internationally  recognized  frameworks  such  as  the  Integrated  Food  Security  Phase  Classification  (IPC)  and  the  Cadre  Harmonisé  (CH) .
Social  Safety  Nets
To  mitigate  food  insecurity  and  support  vulnerable  populations,  the  government  implements  social  safety  net  programs.  The  national  school  feeding  programme  is  a  key  example,  primarily  targeting  primary  school  children  in
rural  areas  where  school  enrollment  is  low  and  food  insecurity  can  be  a  barrier  to  education.
Impact  of  Conflict  and  Displacement
Worsening  insecurity  in  parts  of  the  country  has  had  a  severe  impact  on  the  agricultural  sector.  Conflict  hampers  agricultural  activities  by  preventing  farmers  from  safely  accessing  their  fields,  causes  large-scale  population  displacement  that  disrupts  rural  economies,  and  destroys  livelihoods,  pushing  more  people  into  acute  food  insecurity.
International  and  Regional  Collaboration
A  broad  coalition  of  international  and  regional  partners  provides  critical  financial  and  technical  support  to  Burkina  Faso's  agricultural  sector.  This  collaboration  is  essential  for  funding  key  projects,  shaping  policy,  and  introducing  innovative  solutions  to  the  sector's  challenges.
